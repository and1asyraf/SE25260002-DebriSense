5.4. Critical Evaluation
The critical evaluation highlights the advantages, disadvantages, strengths and limitations of DebriSense by offering a balanced perspective on its effectiveness, efficiency and potential for adoption in real-world environmental monitoring scenarios.

5.4.1. Advantages and Strengths
• Adaptive Knowledge Engineering: DebriSense utilizes a dynamic empirical feedback loop, allowing the Debris Risk Index (DRI) and debris type breakdown predictions to continuously learn and recalibrate based on historical data and actual ground-truth reports submitted by NGOs. This ensures the system becomes progressively more accurate over time.
• Predictive Debris Risk Index (DRI): By integrating real-time external environmental APIs (rainfall, tide, wind speed, water flow), the system proactively forecasts potential debris accumulation, enabling early detection and targeted cleanup logistics before critical blockages occur.
• Interactive Geospatial Visualisation: The platform features an interactive map dashboard that allows users to instantly visualize active hotspots, seamlessly filter by personalized watchlists, and view comprehensive prediction insights (such as doughnut charts) directly on the map interface.
• Community-Driven Scalability: The system includes a robust Location Request module that empowers NGOs to suggest new river hotspots. This crowd-sourced approach allows the system's geographical coverage to scale dynamically while maintaining strict administrator oversight for data integrity.
• Automated Alert System: The system automatically evaluates the calculated DRI and triggers immediate warnings if the risk level reaches 'High' or 'Critical'. These alerts are dispatched to users tracking specific rivers, improving response times and facilitating preemptive action.
• AI-Powered Chatbot Assistance: The integration of the Gemini API provides an interactive, context-aware chatbot that assists users with system navigation and environmental protocols, significantly enhancing the overall user experience.

5.4.2. Disadvantages and Limitations
• Dependence on External APIs: The accuracy and functionality of the DRI calculations are highly reliant on the uptime and precision of external weather APIs. Any downtime, latency, or inaccurate data from these third-party services directly impacts the system's predictive reliability.
• Reliance on NGO Ground-Truth Data: The success of the adaptive learning model requires consistent and accurate manual data entry from NGOs through Hotspot Cleanup Reports. If NGOs submit sparse, infrequent, or inaccurate payload data, the system's debris breakdown predictions may skew.
• Absence of Direct IoT Sensor Integration: Currently, DebriSense relies on macro-level external weather APIs and mathematical baseline profiling rather than physical IoT sensors placed directly in the rivers. This macro approach may limit extreme localized precision compared to hardware-based monitoring.
• User Adoption Challenge: The success of the empirical feedback loop heavily depends on user adoption by local NGOs. Resistance may arise due to unfamiliarity with digital systems, requiring training and a shift away from traditional manual cleanup reporting methods.
